Merit and Focus

We advance forensic, complex system science, and legal and policy innovations, developing and deploying novel strategies and tools throughout the forensic process:

  1. Rapid detection methods to identify potential contraband and establish probable cause for seizure, including remote air sampling to search shipping containers, and use of photo recognition and handheld DNA analysis devices to detect listed species.
  2. Combining genetic, chemical, isotope, pollen, and pathogen data with physical evidence (documents, cell phone logs) to determine the origins, dates, and compositions of contraband and detect links between seized shipments.
  3. Synthesis of diverse information, bolstered by strong data, collaboration, and communication, to build pictures of the responsible criminal organizations, make investigations more proactive, and support integrated cases admissible in courts of law.

Initial work will focus on four areas:

  1. Wildlife trafficking in Africa, where a limited number of large criminal organizations traffic in ivory, pangolin scales, and rhino horn.
  2. Global illegal logging and trade of timber, leading to deforestation and destruction of indigenous communities, and promoting governmental corruption.
  3. Illegal, unregulated and unreported (IUU) fishing, which drive both environmental damage and human trafficking.
  4. Monitoring of pathogens in illicit wildlife and timber products, important both in tracing the origin of shipments and in managing the grave risk of disease transmission to humans, animals, and plants.

The techniques developed, however, will be broadly useful against diverse forms of environmental crime and illicit trade networks. Leveraging collaboration across disciplines brings multiple techniques to bear on stopping environmental crime: physical and biological sciences to detect and analyze contraband; social and data sciences to build predictive models of criminal activities; criminology and law to manage investigations in a legally sound manner.
